1.
 
Background
 
and
 
Introduction
 
 
The
 
digitalization
 
of
 
global
 
finance
 
has
 
exponentially
 
increased
 
the
 
volume
 
and
 
velocity
 
of
 
transactions,
 
bringing
 
with
 
it
 
a
 
corresponding
 
rise
 
in
 
financial
 
crimes
 
such
 
as
 
credit
 
card
 
fraud
 
and
 
money
 
laundering.
 
To
 
combat
 
this,
 
financial
 
institutions
 
heavily
 
rely
 
on
 
Machine
 
Learning
 
classification
 
models
 
to
 
monitor
 
transactions
 
in
 
real
 
time.
 
Despite
 
their
 
high
 
accuracy,
 
automated
 
fraud
 
detection
 
is
 
a
 
strictly
 
safety-critical
 
domain.
 
The
 
cost
 
of
 
a
 
false
 
negative
 
can
 
result
 
in
 
millions
 
of
 
dollars
 
in
 
stolen
 
funds
 
and
 
severe
 
regulatory
 
penalties.
 
Conversely,
 
the
 
cost
 
of
 
a
 
false
 
positive
 
results
 
in
 
frozen
 
accounts,
 
ruined
 
customer
 
experiences,
 
and
 
overwhelmed
 
compliance
 
teams.
 
In
 
this
 
high-stakes
 
environment,
 
simply
 
outputting
 
a
 
binary
 
prediction
 
(Fraud
 
vs.
 
Genuine)
 
is
 
insufficient.
 
Fraudsters
 
continuously
 
adapt
 
their
 
strategies,
 
creating
 
distribution
 
shifts.
 
When
 
ML
 
models
 
encounter
 
these
 
novel,
 
out-of-distribution
 
tactics,
 
they
 
must
 
not
 
only
 
be
 
highly
 
accurate
 
but
 
also
 
well-calibrated,
 
meaning
 
their
 
output
 
probabilities
 
must
 
accurately
 
reflect
 
the
 
true
 
likelihood
 
of
 
correctness.
 
Furthermore,
 
to
 
balance
 
automation
 
with
 
safety,
 
these
 
systems
 
require
 
an
 
integrated
 
"ML
 
with
 
rejection"
 
mechanism.
 
By
 
quantifying
 
predictive
 
uncertainty,
 
the
 
model
 
can
 
automatically
 
approve
 
or
 
block
 
clear-cut
 
cases,
 
while
 
purposefully
 
abstaining
 
from
 
ambiguous
 
transactions
 
and
 
deferring
 
them
 
to
 
human
 
compliance
 
officers
 
for
 
manual
 
review.
 
 
2.
 
Literature
 
Review
 
 
Fraud-detection
 
research
 
has
 
recently
 
optimised
 
heavily
 
for
 
discriminative
 
performance
 
while
 
paying
 
comparatively
 
little
 
attention
 
to
 
whether
 
a
 
model's
 
probabilities
 
can
 
be
 
trusted

it
 
recognises
 
when
 
to
 
stop.
 
Naqvi's
 
[1]

review
 
of
 
work
 
from
 
2020
 
to
 
2025,
 
which
 
screened
 
more
 
than
 
a
 
thousand
 
records
 
and
 
examined
 
seventy-seven
 
studies
 
in
 
depth,
 
makes
 
this
 
explicit:
 
it
 
argues
 
that
 
models
 
should
 
be
 
assessed
 
along
 
an
 
operational-capability
 
axis
 
covering
 
data
 
realism,
 
robustness
 
to
 
drifting
 
fraud
 
patterns,
 
and
 
governance
 
for
 
human
 
investigators,
 
and
 
it
 
reports
 
that
 
probability
 
calibration
 
and
 
the
 
setting
 
of
 
operational
 
decision
 
thresholds
 
are
 
routinely
 
absent
 
despite
 
being
 
essential
 
to
 
real
 
workflows.
 
The
 
review
 
concludes
 
that
 
detectors
 
ought
 
to
 
be
 
paired
 
with
 
explicit
 
decision
 
policies,
 
including
 
the
 
routing
 
of
 
uncertain
 
cases
 
to
 
analysts.
 
A
 
first
 
line
 
of
 
work
 
responds
 
by
 
attaching
 
uncertainty
 
estimates
 
to
 
predictions
 
rather
 
than
 
reporting
 
bare
 
labels.
 
Habibpour
 
et
 
al.
 
[2]
 
are
 
representative:
 
observing
 
that
 
deep
 
networks
 
for
 
card
 
fraud
 
have
 
been
 
tuned
 
for
 
point
 
accuracy
 
while
 
their
 
confidence
 
goes
 
unmeasured,
 
they
 
apply
 
Bayesian-style
 
estimators,
 
Monte
 
Carlo
 
Dropout,
 
and
 
deep
 
ensembles,
 
to
 
quantify
 
predictive
 
uncertainty,
 
and
 
show
 
that
 
the
 
resulting
 
estimates
 
surface
 
borderline
 
cases
 
and
 
let
 
a
 
system
 
abstain
 
when
 
confidence
 
is
 
low.
 
Their
 
motivation
 
is
 
precisely
 
the
 
safety-critical
 
framing
 
adopted
 
here:
 
because
 
fraudsters
 
change
 
tactics
 
continually,
 
the
 
model
 
repeatedly
 
meets
 
data
 
unlike
 
its
 
training
 
distribution,
 
and
 
because
 
expert
 
review
 
is
 
slow,
 
only
 
a
 
small
 
fraction
 
of
 
transactions
 
are
 
ever
 
verified.
 
The
 
practical
 
drawback
 
is
 
cost,
 
since
 
these
 
sampling-based
 
estimators
 
require
 
many
 
forward
 
passes
 
per
 
transaction,
 
awkward
 
under
 
the
 
millisecond
 
budgets
 
of
 
real-time
 
screening.

of

the

This

3.
 
Problem
 
Statement
 
 
Most
 
fraud-detection
 
pipelines
 
are
 
optimised
 
almost
 
entirely
 
for
 
classification
 
metrics
 
such
 
as
 
AUPRC
 
or
 
F1,
 
while
 
predictive
 
uncertainty
 
and
 
calibration
 
receive
 
little
 
attention.
 
Three
 
connected
 
shortcomings
 
follow.
 
First,
 
the
 
probabilities
 
are
 
not
 
trustworthy
 
as
 
probabilities:
 
networks
 
trained
 
with
 
cross-entropy
 
are
 
overconfident
 
[3],
 
so
 
a
 
transaction
 
with
 
novel
 
characteristics
 
can
 
still
 
receive
 
a
 
99%
 
score,
 
and
 
such
 
poorly
 
calibrated
 
outputs
 
(high
 
ECE)
 
are
 
an
 
unreliable
 
basis
 
for
 
automated
 
thresholds.
 
Second,
 
most
 
systems
 
offer
 
no
 
principled
 
way
 
to
 
abstain.
 
They
 
force
 
a
 
binary
 
verdict
 
on
 
every
 
transaction,
 
so
 
the
 
model
 
must
 
guess
 
even
 
with
 
no
 
relevant
 
experience
  
a
 
direct
 
contributor
 
to
 
high
 
false-positive
 
rates.
 
What
 
is
 
missing
 
is
 
the
 
ability
 
to
 
separate
 
epistemic
 
uncertainty
 
(ignorance
 
when
 
meeting
 
OOD
 
tactics)
 
from
 
aleatoric
 
uncertainty
 
(noisy
 
or
 
ambiguous
 
data)
 
and
 
to
 
defer
 
on
 
the
 
former.

4.
 
Proposed
 
Method
 
We
 
propose
 
a
 
framework
 
combining
 
Evidential
 
Deep
 
Learning
 
(EDL)
 
for
 
single-pass
 
uncertainty
 
estimation,
 
post-hoc
 
calibration
 
of
 
the
 
confidence
 
scores,
 
and
 
a
 
selective
 
classification
 
policy
 
built
 
on
 
the
 
estimated
 
uncertainty.
 
The
 
three
 
components
 
target
 
the
 
three
 
shortcomings
 
above
 
and
 
may
 
be
 
refined
 
as
 
the
 
project
 
progresses.
 
An
 
uncertainty
 
quantification
 
with
 
EDL
.
 
Rather
 
than
 
the
 
multiple
 
passes
 
that
 
Deep
 
Ensembles
 
or
 
MC
 
Dropout
 
require,
 
we
 
formulate
 
the
 
classifier
 
using
 
EDL
 
[6].
 
The
 
network
 
outputs
 
the
 
parameters
 
of
 
a
 
Dirichlet
 
distribution
 
over
 
class
 
probabilities,
 
treating
 
predictions
 
as
 
evidence-based
 
opinions.
 
From
 
a
 
single
 
forward
 
pass,
 
this
 
yields
 
an
 
aleatoric
 
component
 
and
 
an
 
epistemic
 
component.
 
The
 
single-pass
 
property
 
is
 
what
 
makes
 
the
 
approach
 
attractive
 
under
 
latency
 
constraints.
 
Post-hoc
 
calibration
.
 
To
 
make
 
the
 
scores
 
usable
 
for
 
threshold-based
 
decisions,
 
we
 
calibrate
 
them
 
after
 
training,
 
primarily
 
with
 
temperature
 
scaling
 
[3],
 
with
 
calibration-aware
 
losses
 
such
 
as
 
focal
 
loss
 
as
 
an
 
alternative.
 
Calibration
 
quality
 
is
 
measured
 
with
 
ECE
 
and
 
the
 
Brier
 
score.
 
Because
 
fraud
 
data
 
is
 
extremely
 
imbalanced,
 
the
 
mass
 
of
 
legitimate
 
transactions
 
can
 
mask
 
poor
 
calibration
 
on
 
rare
 
fraud
 
cases,
 
so
 
calibration
 
is
 
examined
 
on
 
the
 
minority
 
class
 
specifically,
 
not
 
only
 
in
 
aggregate.
 
Selective
 
classification
.
 
We
 
then
 
attach
 
a
 
rejection
 
policy.
 
Let
 
U(x)
 
be
 
the
 
normalised
 
epistemic
 
uncertainty
 
for
 
transaction
 
x
 
and
 
τ
 
∈
 
[0,
 
1]
 
an
 
operating
 
threshold
 
reflecting
 
risk
 
tolerance.
 
If
 
U(x)
 
≤
 
τ,
 
the
 
system
 
acts
 
automatically;
 
if
 
U(x)
 
>
 
τ,
 
it
 
abstains
 
and
 
routes
 
the
 
case,
 
with
 
supporting
 
information,
 
to
 
a
 
human
 
officer.
 
Sweeping
 
τ
 
traces
 
the
 
trade-off
 
between
 
coverage
 
and
 
reliability,
 
reported
 
via
 
accuracy
 
rejection
 
and
 
risk
 
coverage
 
curves.
 
Baselines
 
include
 
softmax
 
with
 
temperature
 
scaling,
 
MC
 
Dropout,
 
and
 
Deep
 
Ensembles,
 
so
 
the
 
contribution
 
of
 
each
 
component
 
can
 
be
 
assessed
 
separately.
